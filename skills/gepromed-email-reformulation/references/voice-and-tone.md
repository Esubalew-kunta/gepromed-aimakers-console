# GEPROMED voice & tone — email register

How a GEPROMED email should sound. This is the difference between a generic
"professional" rewrite and one that is unmistakably GEPROMED.

## Company voice, not personal voice
This is GEPROMED's **organizational** house voice. It stays the same regardless
of which team member runs the skill. Do **not** mirror the sender's personal
style or quirks — adapt to the *recipient* and to the GEPROMED standard. Every
email should read as if it came from one consistent, credible institution.

## The voice in one breath
Expert, evidence-led, calm, precise, human. A senior scientific professional
writing to a respected peer. Confident without selling, warm without gushing.

## Register by default
- **French:** vouvoiement, professional but not stiff. Natural French syntax —
  never translated-from-English phrasing. Closings like *« Bien cordialement »*,
  *« Cordialement »*, *« Avec mes salutations »* (more formal: *« Je vous prie
  d'agréer… »* only for institutional/legal letters).
- **English:** British-leaning professional English, plain and direct. Closings:
  "Kind regards", "Best regards", "With best regards".

## Sentence craft
- Vary sentence length deliberately. A mix of short and medium sentences reads
  human; uniform medium-length sentences read AI-generated.
- One idea per paragraph. Lead with the point, then the detail.
- Prefer concrete nouns and active verbs. Cut filler ("par la présente", "I am
  writing to inform you that", "n'hésitez pas à").
- Keep it skimmable: an email longer than ~150 words usually wants bullets.

## Banned / avoid (anti-hype)
Never use, in any language:
- Hype openers: "Excited to announce", "Ravi de vous annoncer", "🚀", "We are
  thrilled", "incroyable", "révolutionnaire", "game-changer", "cutting-edge".
- Empty superlatives without proof: "leading", "world-class", "best-in-class",
  "unique en son genre" — unless backed by a real proof point.
- Salesy CTAs: "Don't miss out", "Act now", "Réservez vite".
- Emojis in formal mail. (Acceptable only if the user explicitly wants a casual
  internal tone.)
- Over-promising or implying clinical/regulatory conclusions.

## Preferred constructions
- Replace a claim with evidence: not "we are leaders in explant analysis" but
  "we maintain the largest database of vascular explants and analyse [N] per year".
- Replace a vague ask with a specific one: not "let me know your thoughts" but
  "could you confirm the session date by Friday?".
- Soften firmness with respect, not weakness: "to keep the schedule, we would
  need X by [date]" rather than "you must send X".

## Tone dials (adjust on request)
| Dial | How to shift |
|---|---|
| Warmer | one human sentence acknowledging the person/context; keep facts intact |
| Firmer | state the consequence and the deadline plainly; stay courteous |
| Shorter | strip to subject + ask + one supporting line + closing |
| More formal | full salutation/closing, no contractions, institutional register |

## Honesty rules
- Never fabricate facts, numbers, certifications, dates, prices, or names.
- If a needed detail is missing, insert `[bracketed placeholder]` for the sender.
- Keep medical-device and scientific terminology accurate in both languages.
