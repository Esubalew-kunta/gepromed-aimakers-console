# IMRAD structure reference (GEPROMED scientific writing)

How to draft each section of a scientific article. **IMRAD** = Introduction,
Methods, Results, And Discussion (plus Abstract, and often Title/Keywords). Each
section has a distinct job, register, and citation discipline. The skill drafts
the *prose and structure* using **only supplied findings** — it never invents data
or citations.

## The shape of a paper
```
Title
Abstract        (structured or unstructured; a self-contained mini-paper)
Introduction    (why — the question and its context)
Methods         (how — reproducibly)
Results         (what — findings only, no interpretation)
Discussion      (so what — meaning, limits, implications)
References       (human-verified; the skill marks slots, never fills them)
```

## Section-by-section

### Introduction — "why this study"
- **Job:** establish the problem, what is known (with citations), the gap, and the
  specific aim/hypothesis. Funnel from broad context → the precise question.
- **Tense:** present for established knowledge ("X is associated with…"), past for
  prior specific studies.
- **Citations:** dense here — every "it is known that…" needs one. Mark each
  `[CITATION NEEDED]`. **Never** invent the supporting reference.
- **Ends with** a clear statement of aim/objective.

### Methods — "how, reproducibly"
- **Job:** let a competent reader reproduce the work. Design, setting/sample,
  materials/devices, procedures, variables, statistical analysis, ethics/approval.
- **Tense:** past, passive or active per house style ("Devices were tested…").
- **Data discipline:** state only the methods the brief supplies. Do not invent
  sample sizes, instruments, statistical tests, or thresholds — `[bracket]` them.
- **Ethics/approval, registration, and conflict-of-interest** statements: bracket
  for the author; never assert an approval that wasn't supplied.

### Results — "what was found, no spin"
- **Job:** report findings objectively, in the order the question implies. Text +
  references to tables/figures. **No interpretation** (that's Discussion).
- **Tense:** past ("The failure rate was…").
- **Data discipline (strict):** every number, statistic, p-value, CI, and n comes
  **from the brief**. If a value is not supplied, write `[value: to supply]` —
  never generate a plausible-looking statistic. Keep significant figures as given.

### Discussion — "what it means"
- **Job:** interpret results against the question and the literature; state
  implications, then **limitations**, then a measured conclusion.
- **Tense:** present for interpretation, past for what this study did.
- **Hedging (essential):** "suggests", "is consistent with", "may indicate" —
  **never** "proves", "demonstrates conclusively", "establishes" beyond the data.
- **Citations:** comparisons to prior work each need a `[CITATION NEEDED]`.
- **Do not** introduce new results here, and do not over-generalize beyond the
  sample/scope.

### Abstract — "the self-contained mini-paper"
- **Structured** (Background / Methods / Results / Conclusions) or unstructured per
  journal. Usually ≤250 words. Contains the key numbers — **only** supplied ones.
- No citations in the abstract (standard). No claim the body doesn't support.

## Register & integrity rules (all sections)
- **Measured and precise.** Prefer exact nouns and quantified statements over
  adjectives. No hype, no promotional language, no marketing proof points as data.
- **Appropriate hedging** proportional to the evidence. Association ≠ causation —
  keep the distinction.
- **Define abbreviations** on first use; keep device/anatomical terms exact and
  consistent in both languages.
- **Citations are slots, never inventions.** Wherever a reference belongs, write
  `[CITATION NEEDED]`. The skill does **no** citation automation — a generated
  author/year/DOI is a fabricated reference. A human verifies and inserts all
  references.
- **Numbers trace to the brief** or are `[bracketed]`. Never fabricate a statistic
  to make a sentence feel complete.
- **Independence:** neutral tone; no clinical or regulatory conclusion GEPROMED is
  not positioned to make.

## Common FR/EN conventions
- FR scientific prose: present/past per the same logic; « est associé à » not « prouve ».
- Decimal separator and number formatting per the target journal — leave as the
  brief gives them; flag in integrity notes if a journal convention applies.
- Keep tense consistent within a section.
